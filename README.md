# RTW Readiness Prediction in Cancer Survivors
### Predicting Return-to-Work Readiness using Machine Learning

---

## Problem

Cancer survivors often face profound uncertainty when contemplating a return to work. Physical fatigue, cognitive impairment, psychological distress, and unsupportive workplace environments can all combine to make RTW a challenging and poorly understood process. Currently, there is no structured, data-driven tool for healthcare professionals to objectively assess a patient's readiness to return to work.

This project asks: **Can machine learning identify cancer survivors who need additional support before resuming employment?**

---

## Literature Foundation

This project is grounded in 8 peer-reviewed studies spanning breast cancer, colorectal cancer, and prostate cancer survivorship:

| Source | Key Contribution |
|---|---|
| Duijts et al. (2014) | Systematic review: fatigue, cognitive limitations, depression → impaired work ability |
| Islam et al. (2014) | Systematic review: RTW prevalence 43–93%; chemotherapy, fatigue as major barriers |
| Hou et al. (2021) | PHQ-9, BFI, WAI, LASER predictors of employment readiness in Chinese breast cancer patients |
| Ullrich et al. (2022) | Low SES (OR 4.81) and unambitious work behavior (OR 4.48) as strongest long-term RTW predictors in prostate cancer |
| van Maarschalkerweerd et al. (2020) | Barriers shift over time: disease/treatment-related early → personal/work-related later |
| den Bakker et al. (2018) | (Neo)adjuvant therapy, comorbidities → delayed RTW in colorectal cancer |
| Forbes et al. (2024) | Age, comorbidities, health insurance → RTW in Australian cancer survivors |
| Hoving et al. (2009) | Counseling intervention → 76% vs 54% RTW rates; workplace support as key facilitator |

---

## Dataset

A synthetic dataset of **400 cancer survivors** was generated using evidence-based feature weights derived from the literature above. Labels were assigned using a logistic scoring function calibrated to produce realistic class balance (~62% RTW ready), consistent with literature RTW rates.

**Features (18 variables across 4 domains):**

| Domain | Features |
|---|---|
| Clinical | Cancer type, treatment type, disease stage, months since treatment, comorbidities |
| Functional | Fatigue (1–10), pain (1–10), cognitive limitations (1–10), physical functioning (1–10) |
| Psychological | Anxiety (1–10), depression indicator, RTW confidence (1–10), work behavior type |
| Socioeconomic/Work | Socioeconomic status, job type, employer flexibility, employer support, age |

---

## Methodology

### Models
- **Logistic Regression** — interpretable baseline
- **Random Forest** — primary model (robust, handles non-linearity, provides feature importance)

### Evaluation
- 5-fold Stratified Cross-Validation (ROC-AUC)
- Test set: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1
- ROC and Precision-Recall curves

### Interpretation
- Native Random Forest feature importance (Mean Decrease Impurity)
- Permutation importance (model-agnostic, more reliable)

---

## Results

| Model | CV AUC | Test AUC | Test Accuracy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logistic Regression | 0.941 ± 0.018 | 0.974 | 90% |
| Random Forest | 0.892 ± 0.043 | 0.892 | 80% |

> Logistic Regression performed strongly here because the synthetic data was generated by a linear scoring function. In real-world data, Random Forest is likely to be more competitive.

### Top Feature Importances (Random Forest)

1. **Fatigue score** (0.221) — consistent with literature as the single strongest predictor across all 8 studies
2. **Physical functioning** (0.216) — closely related to fatigue; corroborated by Duijts et al. and Hou et al.
3. **RTW confidence** (0.131) — self-efficacy; validated by Hou et al. Work Ability Index
4. **Anxiety score** (0.071)
5. **Pain score** (0.053)

---

## Clinical Insights

| Finding | RTW Ready Rate |
|---|---|
| Fatigue score > 7 | **25.5%** |
| Fatigue score ≤ 4 | **93.6%** |
| Fatigue > 7 AND anxiety > 6 | **14.3%** |
| Work behavior: resigned | 45.2% |
| Work behavior: unambitious | 62.0% |
| Work behavior: healthy-ambitious | 69.4% |

**Key clinical takeaway:** Patients with fatigue above 7/10 combined with high anxiety (>6/10) have roughly a 1-in-7 chance of being RTW ready. These are the patients who need intervention *before* returning to work, not after.

This aligns with Ullrich et al. (2022) finding unambitious and resigned work behavioral patterns as OR 4.48 and 2.77 respectively for not working at 3-year follow-up.

---

## Implications for Care Teams

1. **Screen fatigue and RTW confidence early** — these are the two most actionable, modifiable predictors
2. **Identify work behavior patterns** — unambitious/resigned patterns are detectable during rehabilitation and predictive of long-term failure to RTW
3. **Adjust the intervention over time** — barriers shift (van Maarschalkerweerd et al.): early focus on physical recovery; later focus on workplace accommodation and self-management
4. **Employer engagement is not optional** — flexible hours and employer support are consistent facilitators across all studies; interventions should include employer-facing components

---

## Limitations

- **Synthetic data**: labels are derived from a human-designed scoring formula, not real patient outcomes. This limits the generalizability of specific AUC values.
- **Not clinically validated**: results are exploratory; the model has not been tested on real patient cohorts.
- **Single cultural context**: the literature skews heavily toward Dutch and Western European populations; RTW rates differ significantly in Asian populations (Hou et al.: only 21.35% in Chinese cohort).
- **Static model**: van Maarschalkerweerd et al. show that barriers change over a 5–10 year horizon; a longitudinal model would better capture this.
- **XGBoost unavailable**: network restrictions prevented installation; this would be a natural extension.
